What are Uncommon Approaches?

This website's purpose is to provide simple information to those looking to learn film photography. Since you've now learned all the basic functions, materials, and processes, now it's time to impliment them and start taking your own photos!

The Gallery of Uncommon Approaches serves as a source of insapiritation and information on different techniques to take your photos beyond the basics.



Street Photography

Street photography using film is the art of candidly capturing everyday life in public spaces with a film camera. It emphasizes real, unposed moments and often tells a story about society, culture, or human emotion. Film adds a timeless, gritty aesthetic that many photographers love for its texture, grain, and dynamic range.

Key Techniques

1. Depth of Field:
Shallow DOF can isolate a subject from a busy background, drawing attention. Deep DOF keeps more of the scene in focus, great for layered compositions.

2. Shutter Speed:
Fast shutter speeds freeze motion, ideal for capturing fleeting moments or people in action. Slow shutter speeds can blur movement, but require steady hands or a tripod.

3. Lighting:
Natural light is key. Pay attention to shadows, golden hour light, and contrast for dramatic effects.

4. Subject & Timing:
The best images often capture emotion. Anticipating action and staying ready with your camera settings pre-adjusted is important.



Portraits with Fiber Paper

Black and white film photos printed on fiber paper are a classic and expressive form of photography, known for their rich tonal range, deep blacks, and archival quality. This traditional darkroom process emphasizes craftsmanship and gives each print a unique, tactile feel that digital or resin coated papers can't replicate. fiber based paper, unlike resin coated paper, absorbs chemicals and water, giving a more organic finish and deeper tonal depth. It takes longer to process and dry, but the result is a museum quality, archival print that can last for decades.

Key Techniques

1. Test Strips
Make test strips to find correct exposure by ripping a paper into smaller pieces. This saves a lot of money in the long term. Test print areas with both highlights and shadows Adjust exposure based on test results

2. Dodging and Burning
To fix contrast of specific areas, use dodging and burning. Dodging blocks light to lighten areas, Burning adds light to darken areas. This is done by covering the paper with a flat surface with a hole in it, then shining light through that hole to expose small areas to light.

3. Washing
Washing is so important with fiber paper! Wash in running water for 30-40 minutes, or use Hypo Clearing Agent (HCA) to speed washing. This ensures archival quality and prevents stains

4. Drying and Flattening
Air-dry on screens or line (do not blot), this will take around 24 hours. After that, flatten dry prints between boards or use a print dryer. Fiber curls and warps a lot after air drying so flattening is essential.

Still Life

Still life photography is the art of capturing inanimate objects that are often carefully arranged to create a visually compelling image. It focuses on composition, lighting, texture, and form, allowing the photographer full creative control over every element in the frame. Common subjects include everyday items like fruit, flowers, tools, or personal objects. The goal is to bring life and meaning to these items through thoughtful styling, dramatic lighting, and attention to detail, often evoking mood, symbolism, or storytelling.

Key Techniques

1. Controlled Lighting
Still lives are best shot in a studio or indoors. This can be hard to do with film, so choose a higher iso, or use natural window light or continuous artificial light (like lamps or softboxes). Modify light with reflectors or diffusers to soften shadows and highlight textures.

2. Careful Composition
To make a good still life, apply the rule of thirds, symmetry, or leading lines to guide the viewer's eye. Pay close attention to object placement, balance, and negative space. The placement and position of the items should tell a story.

3. Stable Setup
I would recommend using a tripod for sharp images and precise framing, especially with slower shutter speeds or small apertures. This will help keep consistency if you're changing stuff between shots.

Double Exposure

Double exposure in film photography is a creative technique where two images are combined into one, either during shooting or in the darkroom. The most common method involves exposing the same frame of film twice in the camera. This is done by either manually rewinding the film slightly or using a camera with a built-in multiple exposure mode that allows you to take a second shot on the same frame. To avoid overexposure, each image is typically underexposed by about one stop. Thoughtful planning is keyâ€”areas of shadow in the first image can reveal highlights in the second, creating a balanced and interesting composite.

In the darkroom, double exposures are created by printing two separate negatives onto the same sheet of photo paper. This is done by exposing one negative in the enlarger, then replacing it with a second negative and exposing again, either over the full image or selectively using dodging and burning techniques. This method gives more control over the final composition, allowing the photographer to adjust contrast, exposure, and alignment. Both in-camera and darkroom double exposures can produce surreal, dreamlike images, with the former offering a more spontaneous feel and the latter allowing for precision and experimentation.

Key Techniques



In-Camera Double Exposure

Underexpose Each Shot. Reduce exposure by about 1 stop per image to avoid overexposing the final frame.

Plan Subject Placement. Compose so dark areas in one image allow the second image to show through clearly.

Shoot one round of film focusing on texture, and the second round focusing on form. The order doesn't matter, but by doing this it ensures your photos come out looking dynamic, layered, and not too chaotic.



Darkroom Double Exposure

Balance Exposure Times. Use test strips to find the right exposure for each negative so one doesn't overpower the other.

Use Masks or Dodging. Selectively block or allow light during each exposure to control which parts of each image appear.
