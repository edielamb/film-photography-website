Developing & Darkroom

This page covers how to make unique prints of your negatives using color and B&W darkrooms, along with tips on developing and color management.

A darkroom is a space used to develop and print photographs from film. In black and white (B&W) darkrooms, the film is developed using a series of chemical baths, then images are printed onto light-sensitive paper and processed in more chemicals under red or amber safelights. Color darkrooms are more complex and require total darkness, as color paper is more sensitive to light and the process involves more precise temperature and chemical control. Each type uses enlargers to project the film image onto photo paper before development. Darkrooms give photographers hands on control over the final look of their prints, including contrast, exposure, and tone.

Black & White Darkroom

Enlarger

A black and white darkroom enlarger is what you will use to get your image from a negative to a full sized positive print. The enlarger projects light through a negative onto light-sensitive photographic paper to create a print. It uses a lens and adjustable height to control the size and focus of the image. The amount of light and contrast filters used during exposure affect the final print's brightness and contrast. Read about different parts of the enlarger corresponding with the numbers labeled on the image.

Parts of the Enlarger


1. Lens: The lens on a black and white enlarger focuses the projected image from the negative onto the photo paper.


2. Timer: The timer controls how long the enlarger light stays on, determining the exposure time for the photo paper.


3. Contrast filter: The contrast filter adjusts the image's contrast by controlling how different tones appear in the final print.


4. Paper: The paper is light-sensitive and captures the image projected from the negative when exposed and developed.


5. Easel: The easel holds the photo paper flat and in place, helping frame and align the image during exposure.

Chemicals

The ammount of time your print spends in each chemical can change the outcome of the image. For example, longer developing times will make the tones much richer and highly contrasted vs shorter developing time which will made the image appear faded. Another way to adjust the tonal outcome of your images is to use Contrast Filters. Contrast filters in film darkrooms are used during the printing process to control the contrast of black-and-white photographic prints made from negatives. They adjust how the light exposes the photographic paper. The level of contrast in a filter is catagorized by number, usually ranging from levels 00 to 5, with lower-numbered filters (levels 0-2) producing softer, lower-contrast images and higher-numbered filters (levels 3-5) creating more dramatic, higher-contrast prints. This helps achieve the desired tonal range and emphasize details in highlights or shadows. The general rule of thumb is; higher contrast will make your black tones blacker, and your white tones whiter. Lower contast will do the opposite and make the tones appear in different shades of grey.

In black and white darkroom printing, the chemicals are used in a specific order:




Developer: This brings out the image on the exposed photo paper by reacting with the areas hit by light.



Stop Bath: This stops the development process immediately to prevent overdevelopment.



Fixer: This makes the image permanent by removing any unexposed silver halide, so the paper can be exposed to light without changing.



Water Wash: This rinses off all remaining chemicals to preserve the print and prevent damage or fading over time.


Color Darkroom

Enlarger

A color enlarger is different than a Black & White enlarger because it uses CMYK values to control the color. It also makes prints from color negatives by projecting the image onto color photographic paper. It has built-in adjustable CMYK filters to control color balance and contrast during exposure. By adjusting these filters, you can correct color casts and fine-tune the look of the final print. Read about different parts of the enlarger corresponding with the numbers labeled on the image.

Now in terms of devweloping the image, the color darkroom developing process differs from black and white in that it uses different chemicals specifically designed for color prints instead of the black and white developer, stop bath, and fixer. Color printing requires precise temperature control and timing, which is why it's often done using automated machines that ensure consistency. Unlike black and white printing, which can be done manually under a red safelight, color printing must be done in complete darkness because color paper is sensitive to all light.

Producing color prints at home might be more difficult than bland & white. Although it is totally possible with the right tools and equipment, most people will find it eaier to simply send their negatives to a printing facility or a camera shop that has a fully functioning darkroom. Although they are rare, certain places offer darkrooms for rent, which makes it possible to do the printing process yourself.

Parts of the Color Enlarger

1. Color Dials: These dials control the amount of cyan, magenta, and yellow light to adjust the color balance of the print.


2. Focuser: A small magnifier tool used to ensure the projected image from the enlarger is sharply focused on the paper.


3. Lens: Projects the image from the negative onto the photographic paper and allows for adjustments in sharpness and exposure.


4. Easel: Holds the photographic paper in place and helps align and crop the image for consistent, clean borders.


Not pictured: similar to the black and white enlarger, color enlargers also include timers and require photographic paper (color paper works different than b&w paper! Make sure you use the right one).

Color Values & Fine-tuning your Print

There are three primary colors in negative colour photography: red, green and blue. Inbetween that, there is cyan, magenta, and yellow. Each film emulsion contains three layers of filters sensitive to those colors, and the same applies to colour paper. When those layers are combined they create the full picture.

As each film and batch of paper has different color dominance, you'll need do adjust the color dials to get the colour balance that you want. How do you do that? By using the enlarger's color dials.

The tricky thing about printing color film is that adjusting the color levels works opposite. For example, on the color dials, increasing cyan removes red, increasing magenta removes green, and increasing yellow removes blue. If your image comes out too green, you likely need to ADD magenta, not decrease cyan + yellow. Think of it as a game of addition.
